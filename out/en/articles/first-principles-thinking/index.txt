b:T4b4,<p>The core of first-principles thinking is to decompose a problem into a sufficiently basic layer, then rebuild judgment from foundational facts.</p>
<p>It does not reject experience. It prevents us from being controlled by unverified experience.</p>
<h2>Start with Assumptions</h2>
<p>When facing complex problems, we often jump directly to solutions. But every solution contains hidden assumptions: the goal, constraints, resources, and standards of judgment.</p>
<p>First-principles thinking asks us to make those assumptions explicit.</p>
<h2>Decompose Until It Is Stable Enough</h2>
<p>The basic layer does not have to be mathematically absolute. It only needs to be stable enough for the current problem and the current action.</p>
<p>In career decisions, foundational facts may include personal capability, market demand, industry cycles, family responsibility, and cash-flow constraints.</p>
<h2>Recombine for Action</h2>
<p>After decomposition, the more important step is recombination. Good judgment does not merely break a problem apart. It reorganizes key elements into an actionable path.</p>
<p>This is why Life OS emphasizes analysis, judgment, and action as one integrated practice.</p>
